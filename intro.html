The Upper Tana Nairobi Waterfund

The Upper Tana-Nairobi Water Fund is an initiative bringing together [X, Y, Z] to promote [clean and reliable water supplies for downstream stakeholders, while simultaneously fostering sustainable livelihoods for upstream farmers and others.] The main “on-the-ground” action the fund can take is the identification and prioritization of extension activities, whereby actors on the landscape (mainly farmers) are visited by local NGO’s to diagnose and offer incentives for ways to improve their yields and improve water-related outcomes. Ideally, achieving all these good outcomes simultaneously, though in some places there might be trade-offs, which the Fund is interested in minimizing. Ecosystem services (ES)

Ecosystem services are the benefits natural capital assets provide to humanity

Three related ecosystem services (ES) objectives are being acted upon:

  • * Annual water yield (AWY) to be maximized, as it serves water supply and hydropower needs
  • * Sediment export to streams (SDE) to be minimized, as it harms reservoir and water treatment operations, among others
  • * Soil erosion (SDL) to be minimzed, as it reduces agricultural productivity

Interventions

Objective: Spatially targeting interventions to enhance ecosystem services.

The goal of the Waterfund is to optimize which interventions to undertake in the Upper Tana basin, in order to improve water quality and quantity supplying Nairobi.

Possible interventions are Road mitigation, Agroforestry, Riparian management, Terracing, Reforestation, Grass strips

Draft in construction : The ability to achieve multiple objectives simultaneously while minimizing negative impacts is a function of what is recommended, but also where it is recommended. For example, if one only cares about avoiding sediment in streams, then buffers where agricultural land meets streams may be most effective -- but upland soils could still lose productivity. In this case an intervention that prevents on-field erosion likely will have dual benefits. Yet, at the same time, the increase in yields may reduce water reaching streams as plants utilize additional water. This general problem is one that can be formalized as a “multi-objective optimization” -- ecosystem services models that represent changing management of the landscape can be run in combination with solvers to identify landscape configurations that can approximate the best combination of a given combination of objectives, and also to understand how the recommended configuration changes. Because different actors may have different priorities, and because modeled outcomes can only be modeled imprecisely, it is illuminating to consider how portfolios and their performance change in response to these alternative values and scenarios. By doing so, one can gain insight into relationships and identify recommendations that meet multiple priorities in a variety of possible [future] worlds.

The general priorities:

To begin, we model three related goals, detailed higher in section (ES) For the time being these are represented in biophysical service terms. Other biophysical objectives we are interested in including are dry season baseflow, and translating soil loss into implications for agricultural productivity or yield. Longer term we may also add valuation associated with different biophysical outputs, or transformation of biophysical outputs to other benefit relevant metrics. Viz solution (sliders)

One run of a spatial optimizer answers the question: Given a known weighting on changing objectives (ie, how much increase in sediment export I’m willing to give up for a change in water yield), where are on the landscape are likely to be best places to promote specific activities, and what changes in each objective can I expect to see? This results in two main outputs: One is a “portfolio” that is a set of pixels on the landscape, indicating where to do what activity (given a budget constraint). The second is a score for each objective, that can be visualized on a number of 2-d plots, or a parallel coordinate plot [see X]. When we generate multiple portfolios under different priorities (eg, how much concern is given to sediment, versus water yield), we can trace-out a trade-off curve, under one [scenario/set of assumptions.] We can then use various maps and other statistics to answer questions like:
